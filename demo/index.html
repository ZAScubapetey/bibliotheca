Big Data
Big data is a term used to describe large and complex sets of data that are difficult to manage. It's characterized by its volume, velocity, and variety, and is often referred to as the "three V's":
Volume: The large amount of data that's collected
Velocity: The speed at which the data is created and collected
Variety: The range of data types that are included
Big data is so large and complex that traditional data management systems can't store, process, and analyze it. However, organizations can use big data to gain insights that can help them make strategic business decisions. Big data is used in machine learning, predictive modeling, and other advanced analytics applications.
The definition of big data is constantly evolving, as new data sources and technologies emerge. For example, a single smartphone user can generate around 40 exabytes of data each month, which is a lot for traditional computing systems to handle.
Horizontal and Vertical system integration
Horizontal and vertical integration are business strategies that can be applied to both production and IT systems:
Horizontal integration:
A business strategy where a company acquires a competitor or related business that operates at the same level in the industry. This can help a company expand into new markets, diversify its product offerings, and reduce competition.
Vertical integration:
A business strategy where a company acquires a business operation within the same production vertical, such as a supplier or distributor. This can help a company reduce costs and increase profits, and make it less dependent on suppliers or distributors.
Horizontal integration in a smart factory:
Involves networking between machines, equipment, and production units. This ensures that devices and software can collect and share production data with other systems.
Vertical integration in a smart factory:
Involves networking beyond traditional production hierarchy levels, from the sensor to the business level of the company.
Horizontal and vertical integration in IT systems:
Industry 4.0 increases the volume and velocity of data being collected and analyzed, which can support enhanced levels of horizontal and vertical integration.
Industrial
Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) is a system of connected devices, sensors, and applications that work together to collect and analyze data from industrial operations. IIoT can be used in many industries, including manufacturing, energy management, and pharmaceuticals.
Additive Manufacturing
Additive manufacturing (AM), also known as 3D printing, is a process that creates physical objects by building them layer by layer from a digital model.
Augmented
Augmented reality (AR) is a technology that combines the real world with computer-generated information to create an interactive experience. AR uses hardware, software, and apps to overlay digital content onto the real world, enhancing the user's experience.
Blockchain
Blockchain is a decentralized, public ledger of data that is shared securely across a network of participants. It is a type of distributed database that records transactions in blocks that are linked together in a chain.

Core Technologies of the Fourth Industrial Revolution

Artificial Intelligence (AI)
AI mimics human intelligence to perform tasks such as speech recognition and decision-making. It revolutionises industries by increasing efficiency and reducing human error.
Blockchain
Blockchain ensures secure, transparent transactions and decentralised data management, transforming industries like finance and healthcare.
Internet of Things (IoT)
IoT connects everyday devices to the internet, enabling them to communicate and optimise functions autonomously. It powers smart homes, connected vehicles, and industries.
5G Networks
5G provides faster, more reliable internet connections, supporting advancements like smart cities, autonomous vehicles, and large-scale IoT networks.
